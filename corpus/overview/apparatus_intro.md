# Chamusca 1920 Domestic Hybrid Energy System

## Overview
The Chamusca 1920 apparatus is a rare surviving example of an early 20th-century autonomous domestic energy management system. Originally installed around 1920 in a rural estate (quinta) at Rua Direita de São Pedro 194, Chamusca, Portugal, it provided local generation, storage, and distribution of electricity for lighting and agricultural machinery prior to the arrival of the public electric grid. The system was donated to the Faraday Museum at Instituto Superior Técnico (IST) in March 2023.

## Evolution Phases
The system's architecture evolved through three distinct technological phases, reflecting the broader transition from mechanical to electrical power:
1. **Mechanical Phase (pre-1918):** A Crossley single-cylinder internal combustion engine was installed to drive water pumps for agricultural irrigation, replacing an older animal-driven water wheel (nora).
2. **DC Electrification Phase (c. 1918–1923):** In May 1918, a direct-current (DC) dynamo manufactured by ASEA was coupled to the engine to generate electricity. Between 1921 and 1923, a bank of 60 Tudor open-cell lead-acid batteries was added to provide 24-hour energy storage and availability.
3. **AC Integration Phase (c. 1929):** Following the arrival of the public alternating current (AC) grid in Chamusca, an ASEA three-phase induction motor was added (installed December 6, 1929) to drive the dynamo, relegating the Crossley engine back to purely mechanical pumping duties.

## Historical Scenarios
The simulation platform models three key operational scenarios:
- **Scenario A:** Running the house on batteries (Engine OFF).
- **Scenario B:** Charging batteries using the Crossley engine (c. 1923 configuration).
- **Scenario C:** Charging batteries using the AC Grid and Induction Motor (c. 1930 configuration).
